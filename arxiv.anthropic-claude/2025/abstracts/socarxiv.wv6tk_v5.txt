Title: An Empirical Investigation into the Utility of Large Language Models in Open-Ended Survey Data Categorization
Authors: None
ID: socarxiv.wv6tk_v5
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/socarxiv.wv6tk_v5

How effectively can Large Language Models (LLMs) approximate social scientist
judgment in categorizing open-ended survey responses? This study systematically
compares four zero-shot LLMs—GPT-4o, Claude 3.7 Sonnet, Sonar Large (Llama 3.1),
and Mistral Large—against human annotators using 1,200 responses from the UC
Berkeley Social Networks Study across three question types, generating over
43,200 individual coding decisions in a multi-label classification framework.
Results demonstrate that leading models achieve human-level performance,
exhibiting variation patterns with human annotators similar to the disagreement
levels observed between human coders themselves. GPT and Claude reached 97%
similarity to a sociologist-derived "gold standard" consensus on straightforward
categorization tasks and 88-91% on complex interpretive challenges, while Llama
and Mistral reached 95-96% and up to 87% respectively. Survey response length
emerged as a critical performance factor, with responses under 50 characters
demonstrating 7-11% higher accuracy across all models. While models effectively
detected nuance in simpler tasks, they struggled with responses containing
multiple codes and implicit meanings. Despite high accuracy metrics, some models
produced systematically different social narratives for certain demographic
groups. The analysis also demonstrated that LLMs can serve as quality control
tools, identifying variation in human annotator reliability. These findings
provide a systematic framework for implementing automated LLM coding in survey
research while demonstrating that models work best as supplements to, rather
than replacements for, human judgment in detecting subtle social patterns.
