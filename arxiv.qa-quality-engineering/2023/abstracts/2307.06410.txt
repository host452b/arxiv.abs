Title: Test case quality: an empirical study on belief and evidence
Authors: Daniel Lucrédio, Auri Marcelo Rizzo Vincenzi, Eduardo Santana de Almeida, Iftekhar Ahmed
ID: 2307.06410
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.06410

Abstract:
Software testing is a mandatory activity in any serious software development
process, as bugs are a reality in software development. This raises the question
of quality: good tests are effective in finding bugs, but until a test case
actually finds a bug, its effectiveness remains unknown. Therefore, determining
what constitutes a good or bad test is necessary. This is not a simple task, and
there are a number of studies that identify different characteristics of a good
test case. A previous study evaluated 29 hypotheses regarding what constitutes a
good test case, but the findings are based on developers' beliefs, which are
subjective and biased. In this paper we investigate eight of these hypotheses,
through an extensive empirical study based on open software repositories.
Despite our best efforts, we were unable to find evidence that supports these
beliefs. This indicates that, although these hypotheses represent good software
engineering advice, they do not necessarily mean that they are enough to provide
the desired outcome of good testing code.
