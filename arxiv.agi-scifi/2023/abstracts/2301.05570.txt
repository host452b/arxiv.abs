Title: John Clark's Latin Verse Machine: 19th Century Computational Creativity
Authors: Mike Sharples
ID: 2301.05570
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.05570

Abstract:
John Clark was inventor of the Eureka machine to generate hexameter Latin verse.
He labored for 13 years from 1832 to implement the device that could compose at
random over 26 million different lines of well-formed verse. This paper proposes
that Clark should be regarded as an early cognitive scientist. Clark described
his machine as an illustration of a theory of "kaleidoscopic evolution" whereby
the Latin verse is "conceived in the mind of the machine" then mechanically
produced and displayed. We describe the background to automated generation of
verse, the design and mechanics of Eureka, its reception in London in 1845 and
its place in the history of language generation by machine. The article
interprets Clark's theory of kaleidoscopic evolution in terms of modern
cognitive science. It suggests that Clark has not been given the recognition he
deserves as a pioneer of computational creativity.
