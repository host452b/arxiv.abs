Title: Can Isotopologues Be Used as Biosignature Gases in Exoplanet Atmospheres?
Authors: Ana Glidden, Sara Seager, Janusz J. Petkowski, Shuhei Ono
ID: 2401.15153
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.15153

Abstract:
Isotopologue ratios are anticipated to be one of the most promising signs of
life that can be observed remotely. On Earth, carbon isotopes have been used for
decades as evidence of modern and early metabolic processes. In fact, carbon
isotopes may be the oldest evidence for life on Earth, though there are
alternative geological processes that can lead to the same magnitude of
fractionation. However, using isotopologues as biosignature gases in exoplanet
atmospheres presents several challenges. Most significantly, we will only have
limited knowledge of the underlying abiotic carbon reservoir of an exoplanet.
Atmospheric carbon isotope ratios will thus have to be compared against the
local interstellar medium or, better yet, their host star. A further substantial
complication is the limited precision of remote atmospheric measurements using
spectroscopy. The various metabolic processes which cause isotope fractionation
cause less fractionation than anticipated measurement precision (biological
fractionation is typically 2 to 7%). While this level of precision is easily
reachable in the laboratory or with special in situ instruments, it is out of
reach of current telescope technology to measure isotope ratios for terrestrial
exoplanet atmospheres. Thus, gas isotopologues are poor biosignatures for
exoplanets given our current and foreseeable technological limitations.
