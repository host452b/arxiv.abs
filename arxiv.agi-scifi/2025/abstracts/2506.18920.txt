Title: Signal Use and Emergent Cooperation
Authors: Michael Williams
ID: 2506.18920
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.18920

Abstract:
In this work, we investigate how autonomous agents, organized into tribes, learn
to use communication signals to coordinate their activities and enhance their
collective efficiency. Using the NEC-DAC (Neurally Encoded Culture - Distributed
Autonomous Communicators) system, where each agent is equipped with its own
neural network for decision-making, we demonstrate how these agents develop a
shared behavioral system -- akin to a culture -- through learning and
signalling. Our research focuses on the self-organization of culture within
these tribes of agents and how varying communication strategies impact their
fitness and cooperation. By analyzing different social structures, such as
authority hierarchies, we show that the culture of cooperation significantly
influences the tribe's performance. Furthermore, we explore how signals not only
facilitate the emergence of culture but also enable its transmission across
generations of agents. Additionally, we examine the benefits of coordinating
behavior and signaling within individual agents' neural networks.
