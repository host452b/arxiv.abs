Title: Speaking like ordinary people, representing ordinary people? Language styles, populist attitudes, and voters’ evaluations of political candidates
Authors: None
ID: socarxiv.s2b4y_v1
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/socarxiv.s2b4y_v1

This article examines the influence of political candidates’ language styles on
voter evaluations. Drawing on two preregistered survey experiments conducted
with nationally representative samples of the U.S. population, I explore how the
use of an informal style, often associated with populist communication, affects
voter perceptions across both non-populist and populist speeches. I also
investigate whether an informal style facilitates connections with specific
segments of the electorate, such as individuals holding populist attitudes or
with lower levels of education. After listening to a brief audio recording,
respondents rated a fictional candidate on attributes such as competence,
intelligence, trustworthiness, sympathy, and relatability. They also reported
their likelihood of supporting this candidate. The results show that candidates
do not generally benefit from adopting a more colloquial way of speaking. When
messages are non-populist, language style has little to no effect on voter
evaluations. However, in the context of populist rhetoric, an informal style
leads to more negative assessments of competence, intelligence, trustworthiness,
and sympathy. Contrary to expectations, voters with populist attitudes are no
more likely than others to evaluate positively candidates who use an informal
style. The same holds for voters who identify with the Republican Party.
