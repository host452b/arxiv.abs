Title: Performative Ritualism in African Development: Rethinking Monitoring and Evaluation in the Age of Algorithms
Authors: None
ID: socarxiv.53jxd_v1
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/socarxiv.53jxd_v1

Monitoring and Evaluation (M&amp;E) has emerged as a critical component of
development practice, especially in African contexts where donor legitimacy and
accountability are upheld by demonstrable quantitative impact. Typically seen as
a technical endeavour, M&amp;E is frequently presumed to yield impartial,
objective assessments of societal advancement. This paper contests that
assumption by proposing a philosophical redefinition of M&amp;E as performative
ritualism, a fusion of Judith Butler’s theory of performativity and Victor
Turner’s anthropology of ritual. The analysis employs a critical conceptual
methodology and normative critique. Butler's explanation of performativity shows
how indicators and reports work not just as reflections but also as performative
utterances that have an "impact" through their repeated use in rituals. Turner's
ritual theory shows that evaluation cycles baseline, midline, and endline act as
rites of passage, turning ambiguity into symbolic closure for funders and
policymakers. Post-humanist insights further expand these frameworks,
illustrating how dashboards, algorithms, and artificial intelligence tools now
operate alongside human evaluators, reinforcing evaluative categories through
automated repetition. The findings indicate the cultural and ethical conflict of
these activities within African cultures. Western evaluation rituals focus on
linearity, abstraction, and bureaucratic closure. In contrast, African
communitarian ethics focus on relational accountability, oral testimony, and
cyclical processes of responsibility. The difference shows how theatrical
imported evaluative systems are; it also shows how to recreate ethics by
thinking of evaluation as participatory storytelling and communal debate instead
of a bureaucratic audit. The article finds that monitoring and evaluation
(M&amp;E) is not a neutral tool but a symbolic system that enacts legitimacy and
authority. Thinking of M&amp;E as performative ritualism brings out its cultural
and philosophical sides and makes room for hybrid models that mix computational
tools with community-based, relational practices. The paper provides a novel
framework for examining development evaluation in the algorithmic era, rooted in
African ethical philosophy.
