Title: The Cultural Devaluation of Feminized Work: The Evolution of Occupational Prestige and Gender Typing in the United States, 1900-2019
Authors: None
ID: socarxiv.8q3ca_v1
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/socarxiv.8q3ca_v1

Previous research on job devaluation typically evaluates the potential wage
declines associated with a significant inflow of women into an occupation;
results have been mixed. Few studies, however, examine the cultural mechanism
central to the thesis, where an occupation's  symbolic value in multiple
dimensions \textit{changes} in response to the \textit{dynamics} of its cultural
association with women. This paper proposes a new semantic approach to trace the
devaluation process in American culture, where occupation titles appear in
scholarly and public discourses with varied semantic proximity to gender- and
prestige-signaling phrases over time. Decade-specific occupation embedding
(1900-2019) from 127 billion words of American English across genres and a novel
fixed-effects estimator show a latent cultural bias against women's work, such
that an occupation's general prestige and perceived potency (but not its moral
standing) declines when it becomes increasingly stereotyped as a female job. The
largest penalties are found in lower- and middle-wage occupations, while most
high-wage occupations, despite experiencing large increases in female share in
recent years, are persistently stereotyped as male professions without a
prestige loss. In total, the cultural mechanism of devaluation accounts for 22.4
--25.9\% of the observed negative link between the changes of occupations'
female typing and hourly wages.
