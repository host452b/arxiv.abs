Title: Framing the Latin American nutrition transition in a historical perspective, 1850 to the present
Authors: Jonathan D. Ablard
ID: openalex.W3141857032
URL: https://doi.org/10.1590/s0104-59702021000100012

This paper argues that many of the foundations and trends that led to the rise
in obesity and other diet-related health problems in Latin America began to
develop in the late nineteenth century. The tendency towards presentism in the
nutrition transition literature provides a much abbreviated and limited history
of changes in diet and weight. Whereas medical and nutrition researchers have
tended to emphasize the recent onset of the crisis, a historical perspective
suggests that increasingly global food sourcing prompted changes in foodways and
a gradual "fattening" of Latin America. This paper also provides a
methodological and historiographic exploration of how to historicize the
nutrition transition, drawing on a diverse array of sources from pre-1980 to the
present.
