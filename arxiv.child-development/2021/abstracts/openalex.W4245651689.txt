Title: Ten theses on the shift from (static) text to (moving) image
Authors: Michael A. Peters, E. Jayne White, Elizabeth Grierson, Georgina Stewart, Nesta Devine
ID: openalex.W4245651689
URL: https://doi.org/10.4324/9781003171959-16

De-stabilising pre-existing assertions of object-subject relationships, the
‘philosophy of the image’ has opened up a new era for understanding the nature
of meaning itself, which was to be pursued later in poststructuralist thought.
But, like all philosophies, this is by no means an endpoint. In contemplating
the moving image, it seems impossible, and frankly naive, to ignore its location
or its profound effect in and across popular culture. Increasingly cyberspace
networks are generating a diet of images in everyday and working lives. Social
media sites are proliferating images as identity markers for co-creation,
titillation, exploitation or other instant affects. Current curatorial practice
in the art museums is to add exhibition notes beside images or at the entrance
of the gallery rooms. For the viewers, these notes are helpful in an explanatory
way, by categorising the images historically, socially, even politically.
