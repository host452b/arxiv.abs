Title: Orality and Literacy 25 Years Later
Authors: Paul A. Soukup
ID: openalex.W150895894
URL: https://scholarcommons.scu.edu/comm/92

Given the influence of the series and particularly of Orality and
Literacy—“Ong’s most widely known book; translated into 11 other languages”
(Farrell, n.d.)—this issue of COMMUNICATION RESEARCH TRENDS looks back at
Orality and Literacy: the book, its reception, and its subsequent use in
communication studies. Ong’s work certainly influenced more than communication,
but to attempt to review all of that runs well beyond the possibility of a
focused review. However, TRENDS will attempt to indicate the scope of the
influence of Orality and Literacy with several bibliographies. And so, this
issue also includes a (most likely incomplete) citation bibliography as well
as—in the spirit of Hawkes’s “informative bibliography”—an abridged classified
bibliography of themes introduced in Orality and Literacy
