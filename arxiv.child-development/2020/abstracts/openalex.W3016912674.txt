Title: From Aristocratic to Ordinary: Shifting Modes of Elite Distinction
Authors: Sam Friedman, Aaron Reeves
ID: openalex.W3016912674
URL: https://doi.org/10.1177/0003122420912941

How do elites signal their superior social position via the consumption of
culture? We address this question by drawing on 120 years of “recreations” data
( N = 71,393) contained within Who’s Who, a unique catalogue of the British
elite. Our results reveal three historical phases of elite cultural distinction:
first, a mode of aristocratic practice forged around the leisure possibilities
afforded by landed estates, which waned significantly in the late-nineteenth
century; second, a highbrow mode dominated by the fine arts, which increased
sharply in the early-twentieth century before gently receding in the most recent
birth cohorts; and, third, a contemporary mode characterized by the blending of
highbrow pursuits with everyday forms of cultural participation, such as
spending time with family, friends, and pets. These shifts reveal changes not
only in the contents of elite culture but also in the nature of elite
distinction, in particular, (1) how the applicability of emulation and
(mis)recognition theories has changed over time, and (2) the emergence of a
contemporary mode that publicly emphasizes everyday cultural practice (to
accentuate ordinariness, authenticity, and cultural connection) while retaining
many tastes that continue to be (mis)recognized as legitimate.
