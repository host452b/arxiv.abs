Title: Identity lost and found: Lessons from the sixties scoop
Authors: Raven Sinclair
ID: openalex.W2992973971
URL: https://doi.org/10.7202/1069527ar

The “Sixties Scoop” describes a period in Aboriginal history in Canada in which
thousands of Aboriginal children were removed from birth families and placed in
non-Aboriginal environments. Despite literature that indicates adoption
breakdown rates of 85-95%, recent research with adults adopted as children
indicates that some adoptees have found solace through reacculturating to their
birth culture and contextualizing their adoptions within colonial history. This
article explores the history of Aboriginal adoption in Canada and examines some
of the issues of transracial adoption through the lens of psychology theories to
aid understanding of identity conﬂicts facing Aboriginal adoptees. The article
concludes with recommendations towards a paradigm shift in adoption policy as it
pertains to Aboriginal children.
