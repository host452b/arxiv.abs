Title: Nutrition education and mealtimes: A textual analysis of policies and practices in early childhood education and care services
Authors: Suzanne M. Harte, Maryanne Theobald, Stewart G. Trost
ID: openalex.W2908539339
URL: 

&lt;b&gt;Background&lt;/b&gt; Making consistent healthy food decisions has a
vital role to play in the expectation of healthy living throughout life.
Establishing healthy eating habits early in life is recognised as a cornerstone
to the development of these lifelong habits. The value of harnessing Early
Childhood Education and Care (ECEC) settings as sites for public health
nutrition intervention has been acknowledged as a large majority of children
access ECEC services &lt;b&gt;Objective&lt;/b&gt; This research examines the
uptake and observed textual representation of healthy eating recommendations in
two Australian ECEC centres. &lt;b&gt;Methods&lt;/b&gt; A textual analysis was
undertaken which included identifying textual artefacts and documents that
aligned with, referred to or drew upon the national curriculum, regulations or
nutrition guidelines. Data was collected by photographing all documentation, and
taking field notes. The research team undertook continuous and iterative
analysis guided by a Constructivist Grounded Theory approach to generate and
develop coding of the identified texts. &lt;b&gt;Results&lt;/b&gt; Abundant
textual material was found, including 34 different examples describing policy
and procedure manuals, regulatory documentation, guidelines, checklists,
posters, children’s documentation, tabled routines and online communication and
intranet portals. Key themes emerged across the settings relating to:
communication, the food environment, socialisation, child agency during
mealtimes and opportunities for learning. &lt;b&gt;Implications for
educators&lt;/b&gt; Findings from this study provide a picture of how national
curriculum, regulations or nutrition guidelines are visible in two ECEC
settings. This information will better inform educators and health professionals
seeking to positively influence children’s eating behaviours through the uptake
of policies and practices promoted through regulatory frameworks and guidelines
for heathy eating. &lt;b&gt;Conclusions&lt;/b&gt; New insights were illuminated
of the expectations placed upon early childhood educators to provide a high-
quality learning environment with children at the centre of education and
wellbeing activities, showing inconsistencies in explicit alignment between
documentation and NQS and EYLF guidelines.
