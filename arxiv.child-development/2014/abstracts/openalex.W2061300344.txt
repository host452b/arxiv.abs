In the mid-1980s the term postcolonial first appeared in the scholarly journals
as subtexts in Ashcroft, Griffiths and Tiffin's writings. By the mid-1990s, the
term established itself in academic and popular discourse. Its subjects include
universality, differences, nationalism, postmodernism, representation and
resistance, ethnicity, feminism, language, education, history, place, and
production (Ashcroft, Griffiths & Tiffin, 2004..Key Concepts in Postcolonial
Studies, p.2) . It was born out of people's "frustrations, their direct,
personal and cultural clashes with conquering culture, and their fears, hope,
and dreams about the future and their own identities"(www.nku.edu). It is the
literature that has been created as a voice to the powerless and the poorest
members of the global community. "Postcolonial theory deals with the reading and
writing of literature written in previously or currently colonized countries; a
literature written in colonizing countries which deals with colonization or
colonized peoples. It focuses particularly on: the way in which literature by
the colonizing culture distorts the experience and realities, and inscribes the
inferiority, of the colonized people and on literature by colonized peoples
which attempts to articulate their identity and reclaim their past in the face
of inevitable Otherness" (www.shs.westport.k12.ct.us).
