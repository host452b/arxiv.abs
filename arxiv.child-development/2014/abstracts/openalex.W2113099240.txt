Title: Making an impact: New directions for arts and humanities research
Authors: Ellen Hazelkorn
ID: openalex.W2113099240
URL: https://doi.org/10.1177/1474022214533891

The severity of the global economic crisis has put the spotlight firmly on
measuring academic and research performance and productivity and assessing its
contribution, value, impact and benefit. While, traditionally, research output
and impact were measured by peer-publications and citations, there is increased
emphasis on a ‘market-driven approach’, which favours the bio-, medical and
technological sciences, and has helped reinforce a disciplinary hierarchy in
which arts and humanities research has struggled for attention. This article
charts the changing policy environment across Ireland, the Netherlands and
Norway. It draws on evidence from the HERAVALUE project that studied how
different stakeholders value arts and humanities research; almost 100 interviews
were conducted with representatives from the academy, policymakers and civil
society in these three countries. Although the arts and culture have played a
distinctive nation-forming role, and continue to do so, each country has adopted
very different policy responses towards arts and humanities research.
