Title: The Rise of Universities
Authors: Charles H. Haskins
ID: openalex.W2045349819
URL: https://doi.org/10.55094/holistence.495

Charles Homer Haskins's "The Rise of Universities" traces the origin of the
modern university to the Middle Ages, specifically the 12th and 13th centuries,
driven by the intellectual "renaissance of the twelfth century" which saw a
great influx of new knowledge, including the complete works of Aristotle and
Roman Law. Unlike Roman and Greek institutions, the medieval university was
fundamentally a "society of masters and scholars" (universitas) organized into
academic gilds. It was initially "built of men" rather than possessing
endowments or buildings, with Bologna emerging as a student-controlled center
for civil law and Paris as a masters' corporation preeminent in theology. The
book highlights that the enduring legacy of these institutions is the
fundamental academic structure—including the curriculum, system of examinations,
faculties, and degrees (bachelor, master, doctor)—which established an unbroken
historical tradition for all modern institutions of higher learning.
