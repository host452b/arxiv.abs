Title: First year students' narratives of 'race' and racism in post-apartheid South Africa.
Authors: Kirstan Puttick
ID: openalex.W2233884753
URL: http://hdl.handle.net/10539/11275

The democratic elections in 1994 marked the formal end of apartheid. During
apartheid&#13;\n'race' was, for the most part, a somewhat rigid construct which,
despite many nuances and&#13;\ncomplexities, typically seemed to frame whiteness
as dominant, normative and largely&#13;\ninvisible, and blackness as subordinate
and marginalised. The transformations brought&#13;\nabout in post-apartheid
South Africa have heralded many positive reformations, such as&#13;\nmacrolevel
institutional changes. However, many of apartheid's racialised patterns
of&#13;\nprivilege and deprivation persist and 'race' continues to influence the
identities of South&#13;\nAfricans. Furthermore, an inherent tension exists in
South Africa's social fabric, where ‘race’&#13;\nand racism are often juxtaposed
against narratives of the Rainbow Nation and&#13;\ncolourblindness. This study,
which is framed by critical 'race' theory and social&#13;\nconstructionism, aims
to explore the extent of the fluidity and rigidity of 'race',
racialisation&#13;\nand racialised identities in post-apartheid South Africa by
exploring the narratives of black&#13;\nand white first year students. This
study collected the narratives of seven black and seven&#13;\nwhite first year
South African university students. It was found that South African
youth&#13;\nidentities can be seen to be functioning in relation to and reaction
against both South&#13;\nAfrica’s racialised past as well as its present socio-
cultural context. It was found that the&#13;\nracialised patterns which
characterised apartheid still impact on black and white youth&#13;\nidentity in
contemporary South Africa. For instance, despite the many disruptions
to&#13;\nwhiteness post-1994, it was noted as still being a normative and
dominant construct to&#13;\nsome extent. Similarly, despite attempts to rectify
power imbalances in the new South&#13;\nAfrica, blackness is still constructed
as being somewhat other and inferior. However, many&#13;\nalternative voices
emerged which subverted these narratives, suggesting that identity is in
a&#13;\nstate of flux. Thus, despite the continued influence of apartheid’s
racialised patterns of&#13;\nidentity, shifts and schisms are appearing in post-
apartheid racialised identity, where issues&#13;\nof racialised dominance and
power relations are no longer as clear cut as they once were.
