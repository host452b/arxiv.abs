Title: Unbridling the Tongues of Women: A Biography of Catherine Helen Spence
Authors: Barbara Caine, Susan Magarey
ID: openalex.W2068115391
URL: https://doi.org/10.2307/27508830

Once launched in the 1870s, Miss Spence won acclaim and affection for her 'rare
gifts of speech and intellect'. At home in Adelaide, a reporter was to praise a
sermon he heard her deliver, approving her slight Scots accent, her clear, firm
voice and her unselfconsciousness. Another observer exclaimed over her speech to
a public meeting: 'Does not Miss Spence speak well and clearly'. Abroad, too, in
1893-4, she elicited enthusiasm. Even in the United States, where a woman
speaking in public was not such a rare phenomenon by the end of the nineteenth
century, Catherine Spence's public address at the World Congress of Charities &
Corrections in Chicago gained, she reported, 'a most attentive hearing, and was
frequently interrupted by applause, which is not such a common thing in America
as it is in Australia'. A charismatic speaker, she was: further east she
presented, she was told, 'the finest political address given in Boston for ten
years'.
