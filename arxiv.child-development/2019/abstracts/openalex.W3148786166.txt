Title: On the Politics of Folk Song Theory in Edwardian England
Authors: Ross Cole
ID: openalex.W3148786166
URL: https://doi.org/10.5406/ethnomusicology.63.1.0019

Abstract This article explores how and why a particular vision of folk song
became widely popular during the early twentieth century. Focusing on Cecil J.
Sharp, I show that despite severe criticism from contemporaries, his beliefs won
out as the dominant paradigm for the understanding of folk music. Interrogating
the politics of his theorizing, moreover, I draw out the hitherto neglected
imbrications between folk revivalism and fascist ideology. Seen as dialectical
tools capable of reforming citizens through the expressive contours of their
racial birthright, I argue, collected songs and dances were repurposed in the
service of forging a national socialist consciousness.
