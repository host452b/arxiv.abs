Title: ‘The magic that can set you free’: the ideology of folk and the myth of the rock community
Authors: Simon Frith
ID: openalex.W2169858719
URL: https://doi.org/10.1017/s0261143000000970

Rock, the saying goes, is ‘the folk music of our time’. Not from a sociological
point of view. If ‘folk’ describes pre-capitalist modes of music production,
rock is, without a doubt, a mass-produced, mass-consumed, commodity. The rock-
folk argument, indeed, is not about how music is made, but about how it works:
rock is taken to express (or reflect) a way of life; rock is used by its
listeners as a folk music — it articulates communal values, comments on shared
social problems. The argument, in other words, is about subcultures rather than
musicmaking; the question of how music comes to represent its listeners is
begged. (I develop these arguments further in Frith 1978, pp. 191–202, and, with
particular reference to punk rock, in Frith 1980.)
