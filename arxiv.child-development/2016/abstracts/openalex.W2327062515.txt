Title: The other neighbour paradox: fantasies and frustrations of ‘living together’ in Antwerp
Authors: Anick Vollebergh
ID: openalex.W2327062515
URL: https://doi.org/10.1080/0031322x.2016.1161957

In this paper, Vollebergh investigates the commitment to establishing
intercultural encounters by so-called ‘active’ white Flemish residents in
Antwerp, and their perpetual disappointment with the responses of their
neighbours of orthodox Jewish and Moroccan backgrounds. Instead of viewing these
relationships either as a product of culturalist social cohesion policies, or as
a vernacular ethical achievement that escapes culturalist politics, she argues
that we should understand them through the figure of the Neighbour. Combining
the theories of Emmanuel Levinas and Slavoj Žižek, she suggests that the
neighbourly relation is a paradox in which the Neighbour as a nearby Other
induces both an ethical desire for total openness in the engagement with this
Other, as well as the uncanny sense that his/her Otherness haunts and makes
impossible such an engagement. When viewed in this way, vernacular intercultural
relationships, and the fantasies and frustrations surrounding them, emerge as
the site where residents of multi-ethnic neighbourhoods in postcolonial Europe
engage and struggle with existential and ethical questions of human
interconnection and, especially, the effects of the culturalist inflection that
these questions have gained.
