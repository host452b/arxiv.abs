Title: School Readiness: Insights from Low-income, African American Mothers
Authors: Lilli Melero
ID: openalex.W2611843788
URL: http://hdl.handle.net/2142/95804

Research has shown that low-income, African American children&#13;\nare
disproportionately at risk for not being ready for school. Children&#13;\nwho
are unprepared for kindergarten are at risk of lifetime&#13;\ndisadvantages
(e.g. school dropout, antisocial behavior, premature&#13;\nparenting). Research
also suggests that perceptions of&#13;\nschool readiness vary and that families
and schools may have&#13;\ndifferent viewpoints on what constitutes school
readiness. The&#13;\npurpose of the research is twofold: First we examine to
explore&#13;\nparents’ perspectives of what school readiness means.
Second,&#13;\nwe examine what parents are doing to promote their
children’s&#13;\nreadiness for school. The study draws upon a series of in-
depth&#13;\ninterviews with low-income, African American mothers of
preschool&#13;\nage children transitioning to kindergarten.
Caregivers&#13;\nwere sampled from families using a Head Start program in
an&#13;\ninner-city neighborhood in Chicago. An interpretive
approach&#13;\nguides the analysis of the interviews. N-Vivo, a qualitative
data&#13;\nanalysis program, will be used to aid in analysis and
interpretation,&#13;\nas well as data displays and memos. These strategies
will&#13;\nallow us to identify key themes and patterns among parents.
The&#13;\nstudy of school readiness will make substantive and applied
contributions.&#13;\nMore specifically, the research will add
new&#13;\nknowledge on school readiness from the perspective of
parents,&#13;\nand elaborate on existing knowledge. Our findings will be
particularly&#13;\nrelevant for preschools and elementary schools. These
findings&#13;\nwill determine effective ways for schools and families to
collaborate&#13;\nto enhance children’s readiness for school.
