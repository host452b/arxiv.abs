Title: Fear: Critical Geopolitics and Everyday Life
Authors: Cindi Katz
ID: openalex.W2246871814
URL: https://doi.org/10.4324/9781315582054-6

The author concerns social reproduction, precious parenting and what she calls
banal terrorism, to argue that the contemporary security state and the reign of
trumped-up paranoia. It engenders have not simply altered the spaces and
material social practices of contemporary childhood but have fed into a
burgeoning regime of surveillance. The household mirrors the practices of the
state while softening its future subjects for what the Bush spin-meisters might
call Operation Enduring Watchfulness. The amplified fear along with the moral
panics with which it is associated provide ready means of distraction from the
political economic, social, environmental and personal problems that face
families of children coming of age in the US at present. The regime of parental
hypervigilance has much in common with that of the homeland security state. In
the name of fear the public environment is monitored, bunkered and conspicuously
patrolled while the home is increasingly fortressed.
