Title: Men and meanings of murder: discourses and power in narratives of male homicide in South Africa
Authors: Garth Stevens
ID: openalex.W70999806
URL: http://hdl.handle.net/10500/2014

The extant South African literature base on male homicide is relatively small
and reveals a&#13;\npaucity of qualitative studies. This study aimed to elicit
discourses embedded within the&#13;\nnarratives of men involved in homicidal
encounters, and to analyse them from a social&#13;\nconstructionist perspective.
Semi-structured, individual interviews were conducted with 30&#13;\nmale
prisoners who were convicted of murder. An analysis of narrative forms, followed
by a&#13;\ncritical discourse analysis of the narrative contents, was conducted
and aimed to assess the&#13;\nsocial and ideological significance, functions and
effects of these discourses. Participants'&#13;\ntalk included masculine
performances that allowed for positive self-presentation and ways
of&#13;\nconstructing meaning of their actions for themselves, the interviewer
and an `invisible&#13;\naudience'. Narrative forms of stability/continuity,
decline, and transformation/growth that&#13;\nrelied on normalising, reifying,
tipping point, propitiatory and rehabilitatory lexical registers&#13;\nwere
deployed as a means to position participants as reasonable, normal,
rehabilitated, and as&#13;\n`successful' men. Within the narrative contents,
participants constructed homicide through&#13;\nexculpatory and justificatory
discourses to rationalise and minimise their agency, and drew&#13;\non
essentialist, moral and deterministic notions of male violence. Discourses of
spectacular&#13;\nand instrumental violence were also evident. References to
male honour, status and power; a&#13;\ndefence against emasculation; the
assertion of control over commodified female partners; the&#13;\nmaintenance of
referent familist and ageist discourses; and the normalisation of
male&#13;\nviolence as a utilitarian tool to access resources in unequal social
contexts, underpinned these&#13;\ndiscourses. The homicidal acts thus
represented adapted performances of hegemonic&#13;\nmasculinity in a noxious
context where this dominant form of masculinity is often&#13;\nunattainable.
While participants' talk reproduced hegemonic constructions of
masculinity&#13;\nwithin broader social contexts, it also contested hegemonic
orders of moral discourses that&#13;\ngovern the legitimacy or illegitimacy of
violence. The findings reveal how contexts of&#13;\ndiscoursal production have a
contradictory response to violence - denouncing it, but
also&#13;\nsimultaneously acting as a pernicious incubatory environment for male
homicide. It&#13;\nconcludes that the prevention of male homicide must involve
the de-linking of masculinities&#13;\nand violence at material, structural and
institutional levels, but also within systems of&#13;\nsignification, if non-
violent masculinities are to gain ascendancy.
