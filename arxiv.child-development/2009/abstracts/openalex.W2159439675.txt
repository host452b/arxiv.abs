Title: The role of Rap/Hip Hop music in the meaning and maintenance of identity in South African youth
Authors: Dror Cohen
ID: openalex.W2159439675
URL: http://hdl.handle.net/10539/6628

Although music has seemingly always formed an integral part of human
culture,&#13;\ntechnological advances in contemporary society have increased
both its accessibility&#13;\nand portability, allowing for unprecedented
production and consumption of a medium&#13;\nthat allows individuals to enact
and display various social identities during day-to-day&#13;\nlife. Furthermore,
recent research has demonstrated that youth consume more music&#13;\nthat any
other age group. Thus music may be considered as a primary
cultural&#13;\ninfluence in the lives of youth. While the bulk of the research
conducted in&#13;\nunderstanding the form and function of this influence has
been located in the&#13;\ndisciplines of sociology and musicology, Psychologists
in Europe and America have&#13;\nbecome increasingly interested in understanding
the role of music in constructing and&#13;\nmaintaining identity during this
critical period of development. As a contribution to&#13;\nthis field of
application outside of these contexts and located within a
qualitative&#13;\nframework, this study explored the role of Rap/Hip Hop music,
as one of the most&#13;\npopular global and local genres of music, in the
meaning and maintenance of identity&#13;\nin a cohort of South African youth.
The resultant thematic framework illustrated the&#13;\ncomplex tensions
negotiated by youth through assuming Hip Hop culture membership&#13;\nin South
Africa. Importantly, the study showed that the nature of Hip Hop culture;
its&#13;\nemphasis on self-expression, individuation and critical social
awareness dovetails&#13;\nwith many of the traditional psychological
developmental theories of youth identity.&#13;\nHip Hop consumption also implied
appropriating identity markers from a wide range&#13;\nof social influences,
posing challenges to the application of traditional social identity&#13;\ntheory
in accounting for in and out groupings. This was most pronounced in the
way&#13;\nthat ‘remixing’, as a governing musical principle in Hip Hop seems to
resonate as key&#13;\nmode of identity and identification amongst its South
African consumers. Thus, it&#13;\nseems fitting that South African youth
currently in the midst of cultural, economic and&#13;\npolitical transitions
would embrace an eclectic rather than rigidly bounded genre of&#13;\nmusic with
such enthusiasm. In some ways then Hip Hop in South Africa, appears
to&#13;\nprovide youth with the means to remix past and present, old and new,
global and&#13;\nlocal, self and other.
