Title: Neoliberalism and Global Cinema
Authors: Lim, Bliss Cua
ID: openalex.W589526264
URL: https://doi.org/10.4324/9780203813638

Committed to exposing the ways in which the personal is always crosshatched with
the social and the political, Filipino filmmaker Jeffrey Jeturian’s traces the
relentless conversion of all forms of social experience into economic
transactions. Whether in Pila Balde [Fetch a Pail of Water]) or Kubrador [Bet
Collector], 2006) Jeturian’s films thematize the neoliberal transformation of
social individuals into calculative entrepreneur-consumers. This chapter traces
two levels at which both films operate to expose the workings of neoliberal
rationality in the Philippines: first, a diegetic elaboration of commodification
in story worlds where sex, longing, death, and grief become subject to economic
calculation; and second, the extra-cinematic dimensions of Filipino film
production in the context of economic crisis and political corruption. My
consideration of Pila Balde, a low-budget pito-pito film, indexes the desperate
measures Philippine film studios adopted to weather the 1990s “crisis of
Philippine cinema” brought on by the Asian economic crisis. In turn, Kubrador
was released in the context of political scandals in 2005 that implicated the
First Gentleman of the Philippines, Mike Arroyo, in payoffs from the illegal
gambling trade, jueteng.
