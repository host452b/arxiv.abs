Title: Public and private spaces in adolescent girls' lives : school graffiti, sexualities and romantic relationships
Authors: Joanne Cassar
ID: openalex.W2582524406
URL: http://etheses.whiterose.ac.uk/14907/2/444280_disc/Chp%201-4%20Graffiti%201-24.PPT

The thesis investigates a number of girls' writings as they occurred in the form
of&#13;\ngraffiti in a sixth-form College in Malta. It develops an analysis of
these graffiti texts,&#13;\nwhich are mainly concerned with how their authors
try to make sense of their gender&#13;\nidentity and involvement in sexual
activity and romantic encounters. The analysis&#13;\nexamines possible reasons
why a number of female students resort to writing graffiti.&#13;\nIt highlights
the ways through which the acquisition of sexual knowledge
occurs&#13;\ninformally outside the formal curriculum, through spaces created
and struggled over&#13;\nby the informants. The study interrogates the
emotional, social, cultural, personal and&#13;\npolitical worlds as described by
them. This includes a discourse analysis, aimed at&#13;\nproviding a deeper
understanding of ignored emotional and social perspectives. In the&#13;\nabsence
of sexuality and relationships education in postsecondary education
curricula&#13;\nin Malta, this study challenges silences surrounding these
matters. The study&#13;\ndocuments the graffiti as subversive processes of
learning, which reproduce and resist&#13;\ndominant discourses. It regards the
graffiti as constituting a discourse in itself,&#13;\nunderstood as possibly
promoting agency in some of the informants' lives.&#13;\nA reflexive approach
was adopted to arrive at the research aims. Poststructuralist&#13;\nfeminist and
queer theory perspectives locate the study's theoretical
positionings.&#13;\nEthnographic observations and informal conversations with
nineteen female students&#13;\nwere employed to assist the data
analysis.&#13;\nThe findings show that some informants question the ways they
relate to their gender&#13;\nidentity when confronted by hetero, lesbian and
bisexual social relationships.&#13;\nHeteronormative dominance and familiarity
are actively reproduced, contested,&#13;\ndisturbed and resisted. The findings
suggest that the informants seek a safe school&#13;\nenvironment, where they can
discuss sexualities and relationships' matters in a&#13;\ncontext of empathy,
caring, understanding and support. They request detailed&#13;\ninformation about
decision-making processes related to conflicting emotions about&#13;\nromantic
feelings, relationships and interpersonal communication skills with
their&#13;\npartner/so&#13;\nThe study points to the need for a deeper
understanding of the emotional and overall&#13;\nwellbeing of teenagers with
respect to romantic and sexual relationships. The study&#13;\naims to contribute
towards academic debates and knowledge about teenage&#13;\nperspectives on
sexualities and romantic relationships and towards the
planning,&#13;\ndiscussion and design of future postsecondary sexuality
education curricula in Malta.
