Title: Controller, Hand, Screen
Authors: Graeme Kirkpatrick
ID: openalex.W1996402494
URL: https://doi.org/10.1177/1555412008325484

This paper tries to clarify the place of the handheld controller in computer
game aesthetics. It starts from the premise that aesthetic form, perhaps the
central category of modernist critical theory, is present in our play with
computer games. The central argument is that controllers and our use of them are
repressed in gameplay and that this repression facilitates a diversion of the
player's energy that helps explain the compulsive nature of good games. Our
sense of participation in events in game fiction is bought at the price of a
loss of interest in our hands. The smooth integration of players into the rough,
faltering world of gameplay is made possible by an excess of energy that passes
from the unacknowledged tension in the hand into the imaginary relation we have
with on-screen action.
