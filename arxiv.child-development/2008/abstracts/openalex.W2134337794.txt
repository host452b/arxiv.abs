Title: PARENTAL SUPPLY AND OFFSPRING DEMAND AMONGST KARO BATAK MOTHERS AND CHILDREN
Authors: Geoff Kushnick
ID: openalex.W2134337794
URL: https://doi.org/10.1017/s0021932008002988

The resolution of parent-offspring conflict (POC) might sway in favour of the
offspring if the parent relies on offspring-supplied information about need.
Here, three hypotheses from a resolution model of POC were tested using data on
sickness histories and mother-infant interactions from 24 Karo Batak women and
their young children from two rural villages in North Sumatra, Indonesia. First,
as predicted, offspring with greater need (measured as age and propensity to
illness) tended to fuss more often. Second, as expected, observed fussiness
predicted the number of suckling occurrences observed during sampling periods.
Third, contrary to the prediction, the duration of fussing observed after
breast-feeding occurrences was longer than the duration of the breast-feeding
occurrences themselves. Parental decisions were made based on offspring-supplied
information about need, but offspring failed to garner resources in excess of
the parental optimum. This suggests that a POC interpretation is unnecessary to
account for these results.
