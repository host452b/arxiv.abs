Title: Journalism students and intergenerational change in journalism
Authors: Penny O’Donnell
ID: openalex.W268904778
URL: http://hdl.handle.net/10453/1511

This article presents a response to the question: "Is there more
to&#13;\njournalism education than workforce reproduction
through&#13;\nsocialisation to the profession and, if so, what is it?"
The&#13;\nresponse is developed in two parts. The first presents the
findings&#13;\nof a 2001 study of University of Technology, Sydney
journalism&#13;\nstudents, their career trajectories and approaches to
professional journalism&#13;\npractice. That study provided the means to
identify&#13;\ndifferent types of students and career aspirations. It found
"confident&#13;\npractice" was a highly prized yet previously
unexplored&#13;\neducational outcome. This finding, in turn, pointed to the need
to&#13;\nreconsider the nature of the relationship between the
university&#13;\nsystem and the news media. The second part of the article
canvasses&#13;\nexisting Australian ideas about professional education
in&#13;\njournalism before introducing new theoretical resources
that&#13;\noffer a more robust means of considering the purpose and
significance&#13;\nof the educational endeavour in relation to both
reproduction&#13;\nand change in journalism. The article argues that the
discussion&#13;\nof intergenerational change in journalism needs to
include&#13;\nthe viewpoints of journalism students as key stakeholders in
journalism's future, and be mindful of the full gamut of their
career&#13;\naspirations, the wide range of innovative and critical
educational&#13;\ninitiatives found in the journalism curricula they study, and
the&#13;\neducational outcomes they prize most highly.
