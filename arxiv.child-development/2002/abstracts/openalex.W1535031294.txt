textabstract“Don’t think of them as terrorist states. Think of them as terrorist
markets.” Thus\nreads a cartoon published in The New Yorker in May 2003.1 The
picture depicts a\nboardroom or seminar room. Seven people have gathered around
a shiny\nconference table and brought notebooks, coffee, soft drinks, a cellular
phone, and a\nlaptop computer. Their attire is formal and office-like. The men
are wearing\njackets and ties, the women prim jerseys or dresses, and all have
conventional\nhaircuts. Almost all are donned with glasses. The room exudes
power: its windows\nreach from floor to ceiling, there is art on the wall, the
table is ostensibly big, the\nswivel chairs seem comfortable, and there is ample
space. The person chairing the\nmeeting is sitting at the top of the table in a
bigger, executive type of chair.\nOutside, through the window, one can half see
the top of the capitol building, the\nseat of Congress, flying a flag. The
participants are all turned toward the person\nsitting at the head of the table,
with their mouths closed. They are listening in an\nattentive, somewhat
subservient manner, but appear open and dignified. The\nperson that presides
over the meeting is more senior. He is speaking – his mouth is\nopened – with
his arms crossed. He sits back however, leaning against the chair.\nHis notes
lie untouched in front of him. He speaks to no one in particular, but
with\nauthority and poise he states: “Don’t think of them as terrorist states.
Think of\nthem as terrorist markets.”
