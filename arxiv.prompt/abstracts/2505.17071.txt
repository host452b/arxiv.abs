Title: What's in a prompt? Language models encode literary style in prompt
embeddings
Authors: Raphaël Sarfati, Haley Moller, Toni J. B. Liu, Nicolas Boullé,
Christopher Earls
ID: 2505.17071
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.17071

Abstract:
Large language models use high-dimensional latent spaces to encode and process
textual information. Much work has investigated how the conceptual content of
words translates into geometrical relationships between their vector
representations. Fewer studies analyze how the cumulative information of an
entire prompt becomes condensed into individual embeddings under the action of
transformer layers. We use literary pieces to show that information about
intangible, rather than factual, aspects of the prompt are contained in deep
representations. We observe that short excerpts (10 - 100 tokens) from different
novels separate in the latent space independently from what next-token
prediction they converge towards. Ensembles from books from the same authors are
much more entangled than across authors, suggesting that embeddings encode
stylistic features. This geometry of style may have applications for authorship
attribution and literary analysis, but most importantly reveals the
sophistication of information processing and compression accomplished by
language models.
